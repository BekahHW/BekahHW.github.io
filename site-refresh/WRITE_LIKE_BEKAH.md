# WRITE_LIKE_BEKAH: Comprehensive Writing Style Blueprint

> **Use this skill when:** writing blog posts, essays, LinkedIn posts, or social content authored by Bekah Hawrot Weigel. Two voice eras inform this guide — recent AI-focused work on bekahhw.com (2026) and earlier open-source community work on opensauced.com (2024). When in doubt, prefer the recent patterns in **Part 7**, which reflect her current voice.

## Part 1: Style Analysis (Pattern Findings)

### Opening Hooks

**Pattern: Contextual Tension Through Anecdote, Question, or Three-Beat Setup**

Observed opening strategies:
1. **Three-beat anecdote → pivot** (dominant in recent AI work, 2026)
   - Beat 1: personal/concrete scene. Beat 2: a short complication or fact. Beat 3: pivot to the post's real subject.
   - Example: "This weekend, my daughter ran in her first high school track meet. One of the other girls' relay teams was disqualified for dropping the baton. It's kind of like what's happening when AI tools communicate." (how-ai-tools-communicate)
   - Example: "Once I was in an interview, and I was asked what motivated me. My answer was momentum. And maybe that's why working with AI can be so engaging sometimes." (hidden-ai-work)
   - Example: "As the Director of Alignment at Meta Superintelligence Labs, Summer Yue's job is keeping AI aligned with human values. Before that, she was at Google DeepMind and Scale AI. If anyone would know how to keep an AI agent in check, it's her." (ai-security-101)

2. **Declarative setup + immediate sharpening**
   - Pattern: opener states the situation, second sentence sharpens it in 3-6 words, third sentence pivots.
   - Example: "Products launching from solo indie hackers who don't know how to code isn't rare anymore. It's the new normal. With this generation of new builders, it means creativity and expertise are no longer bottlenecked by technical skill." (learning-to-work-with-agents)

3. **Story-driven tension** (dominant in opensauced era, 2024)
   - Example: "In May 2022, Dane Springmeyer... announced his decision to step down" (no-maintainers.md)
   - Example: "Hoare lived on the 21st floor, and as he climbed the stairs, he got annoyed" (self-selection.md)
   - Example: "During the pandemic, I occupied the same spacea Zoom roomwith members of the Virtual Coffee community" (when-local-went-global.md)

4. **Provocative question**
   - Example: "What if the primary value of open source isn't in producing software, but in producing better developers?" (hidden-benefits.md)
   - Example: "How many humans could screw up my dependency graph?" (human-dependency.md)

5. **Bold claim/reframe**
   - Example: "Transparency is the New Open Source Currency" (pearai.md)

**Never:** Generic introductions ("In today's world of AI..."), jargon-heavy openers without context, self-referential "in this post I will...", AI-hype openings ("AI is changing everything...").

### Paragraph Structure & Rhythm

**Pattern: Short + Long + Short Cadence**

- Average paragraph length: 2-4 sentences
- Strategic use of single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis (10-15% of paragraphs)
- Longer analytical paragraphs (4-6 sentences) reserved for complex explanations
- Frequent use of transitional paragraphs (1-2 sentences) to shift topics

**Example from no-maintainers.md:**
```
Short: "This situation is just one that captures the challenges..."
Long: "When key maintainers leave, projects can quickly become outdated..."
Short: "Single points of failure can have widespread repercussions."
```

### Sentence Length Variation

**Pattern: Measured + Rhythmic**

- Average sentence: 15-22 words
- Strategic short sentences (3-8 words) for emphasis: "It's terrifying. It's vulnerable. And it's incredibly powerful."
- Occasional longer sentences (25-35 words) for context-building
- Avoids extremely long sentences (40+ words)
- No run-on sentences masquerading as style

### Punctuation Signatures

**Pattern: Em-dash dominant, strategic parentheticals, minimal semicolons**

1. **Em dashes ():** Used 2-4 times per 3000 words
   - Purpose: Add nuance, insert aside, create breath
   - Example: "You might argue that quantifying contributions could lead to a new form of elitism in open source. You're not entirely wrong."

2. **Parentheticals:** Used sparingly (1-2 per 1000 words)
   - Purpose: Acknowledges reader's likely thought or adds quick context
   - Example: "(See: You Don't Need a Mentor: Embracing the Power of Community.)"

3. **Semicolons:** Rare (less than 1 per 2000 words)
   - Preference for periods over semicolons

4. **Strategic italics:** For emphasis on specific words, not phrases
   - Example: "When we *assume* positive intent..."
   - Example: "it's about what you're doing *now* in open source"

### Technical vs Narrative Balance

**Pattern: 60/40 narrative-to-technical**

- Opens with narrative/story (first 2-3 paragraphs)
- Transitions to technical content with context maintained
- Returns to human element in conclusion
- Technical information is always contextualized with "why it matters"
- Avoids jargon dumps; defines terms inline

**Example structure (pearai.md):**
1. Story of what happened (narrative)
2. What the technology/licenses mean (technical)
3. Why it matters to humans (narrative + technical synthesis)

### Use of Metaphor

**Pattern: Grounded, occasional, never overwrought**

- 1-2 metaphors per post maximum
- Metaphors drawn from everyday life: plates, gardens, coffee shops, dairy aisles
- Self-aware about mixing metaphors: "And now I'm going to do my favorite thing and mix metaphors"
- Never extends metaphors beyond their usefulness

**Examples:**
- "You're carrying a lot of plates" (motherhood post)
- "You have to learn how to weed your garden" (immediately after plates metaphor, with self-awareness)
- "The dairy aisle was a Zoom room" (pandemic community post)

### Second Person vs First Person

**Pattern: Strategic code-switching**

1. **Second person (you):** 70% of posts
   - Used for: instructional content, community-facing posts, empowerment messaging
   - Example: "You don't need to immediately respond..."

2. **First person (I/we):** 30% of posts
   - Used for: personal reflection, community leadership, vulnerable moments
   - Example: "I see you moms. You're carrying a lot of plates."
   - Example: "We're not always going to get things right."

3. **First person plural (we) for collective experience:** 15% of posts
   - Example: "We were strangers from different continents creating something..."
   - Purpose: Build shared identity, acknowledge collective challenge

### CTA Style

**Pattern: Soft-to-strategic, never aggressive**

1. **Invitation-based** (50%)
   - "Share your OSCR and your story on social media and tag us"
   - "Join the OSCR Conversation"

2. **Question-prompted** (30%)
   - "What does your OSCR say about your open source contributions?"
   - "Can we build sustainable models...?"

3. **Declarative next-step** (20%)
   - "Make pull requests a non-negotiable part of your workflow"
   - "Use your privilege"

**Never:** Hard selling, urgency manipulation ("Act now!"), FOMO tactics

### Evidence Style

**Pattern: Data + human story, always contextualized**

1. **Quantitative evidence:** Always linked to source, explained for laypeople
   - Example: "60% of maintainers remain unpaid" + link to Tidelift report
   - Example: "Two-thirds of adults reported experiencing social isolation" + link

2. **Qualitative evidence:** Direct quotes with context
   - Example: Full quote from Fred K. Schott + video embed + explanation of significance

3. **Visual evidence:** Screenshots, charts referenced with alt text considerations
   - Used sparingly, always with textual explanation

**Evidence hierarchy:**
- Primary: Human stories, quotes from practitioners
- Secondary: Data/metrics with interpretation
- Tertiary: Visual proof

### How Authority Is Signaled

**Pattern: Earned credibility through lived experience, not credentials**

1. **Through action:** "I've been spending a lot of time engaging with different types of communities..."
2. **Through vulnerability:** "When we created this script, I thought... And I'm so incredibly thankful that someone was willing to tell us..."
3. **Through community:** "Virtual Coffee has been through a progression... we've gotten it wrong"
4. **Through synthesis:** Connecting multiple sources, not just citing them

**Never:** Appeals to credentials, name-dropping without context, assumed authority

### How Disagreement/Nuance Is Expressed

**Pattern: Acknowledge validity, then reframe**

1. **Validation-first disagreement:**
   - "You might argue that quantifying contributions could lead to a new form of elitism in open source. You're not entirely wrong. But the alternative... is worse."
   - "Others will say that open source should be about passion, not recognition. To them, I ask: why not both?"

2. **Tension acknowledgment:**
   - "This shows a fascinating tension in open source contribution: the disconnect between public activity and private effort."

3. **Complexity embrace:**
   - "The answer is a bit more nuanced than a simple yes or no."

**Never:** Dismissive language, strawman arguments, false dichotomies without acknowledgment

### Repeated Phrases & Linguistic Fingerprints

**Documented patterns (frequency per 10,000 words):**

1. **"Here's the thing"** (3-4 times) - signals pivot to core insight
2. **"Let's be clear"** (2-3 times) - precedes boundary-setting
3. **"To be honest" / "Honestly"** (2-3 times) - signals vulnerability or emphasis
4. **"The truth is"** (1-2 times) - reframe after conventional wisdom
5. **"It's not just about X; it's about Y"** (2-3 times) - complexity addition
6. **"What if..."** (1-2 times) - provocative reframe
7. **"But hold that thought"** (1 time) - attention retention
8. **"And yet"** (2-3 times) - contradiction introduction
9. **Question clusters** - 2-3 questions in sequence for rhythm

**Sentence-level signature patterns (NEW — dominant in 2026 AI-era posts):**

10. **Negation-then-reframe** ("X isn't Y. It's Z."): the single most characteristic sentence shape in her recent work. Use 2-4 times per post.
    - "An agent isn't a mind. It's a very well-read improviser."
    - "Fluency is not correctness."
    - "Security isn't one thing. It's a set of questions you ask before you let something happen."
    - "You are the accountable one. Not the tool."
    - "Recognition isn't just a feel-good factor; it's a fundamental human need."
    - "It's not about lifetime achievements; it's about what you're doing now in open source."

11. **"Right up until it doesn't"** suspension construction — names something working, then lands the failure mode.
    - "That's how most people use these tools. And it works, right up until it doesn't."

12. **Cause-effect telegraphic chain** — short clauses, comma-strung, no subordination.
    - "It moves them. Your app stops working, and you don't know why."
    - "You send a prompt, you get text back, you pass it to the next step."
    - "The LLM goes looking for name and finds nothing. The LLM goes looking for question and finds nothing."

13. **Conceptual pair as title or anchor** — pits two terms against each other.
    - "Momentum vs. Alignment Tax", "Fluent vs. correct", "Builder vs. developer", "Intent → output"

14. **Number-grounded directive** — specific count plus a do/stop verb.
    - "If you've been going back and forth more than three times on the same bug, stop."
    - "Three tools at two seconds each is six seconds minimum, plus overhead."

15. **Self-aware non-expertise admission** as a credibility move.
    - "I'm not a security professional."
    - "I am not a designer."
    - "I don't know much about track."
    - Function: disarms readers, establishes humility, then earns authority through what follows.

**Structural fingerprints:**
- Frequent use of colons to introduce explanations.
- Lists formatted as numbered or bulleted, never inline.
- Headers in opensauced-era posts are declarative (80% of the time).
- **Headers in bekahhw.com-era posts skew toward fragments and direct questions** ("What the agent actually is", "Where's your trust dial right now?", "Is this ready?", "The loop you're actually in", "Why I included this one"). Treat fragment/question headers as the default for AI-era content.

## Part 2: Emotional & Confidence Calibration

### Emotional Tone Spectrum

**Primary tones by post type:**

1. **Advocacy posts** (sustainability, privilege, community support)
   - Tone: Measured urgency + empathy
   - Example: "Dear Senior Devs, Use your privilege"
   - Emotional range: Concerned � Resolute � Hopeful

2. **Technical analysis posts** (metrics, tools, trends)
   - Tone: Analytical curiosity + accessible authority
   - Example: "The Human Dependency Graph"
   - Emotional range: Questioning � Explanatory � Strategic

3. **Personal/Community posts** (mom post, imposter syndrome, assume best intent)
   - Tone: Vulnerable � Supportive � Empowering
   - Example: "Moms, you're carrying a lot of plates"
   - Emotional range: Witnessing � Understanding � Encouraging

4. **Provocation posts** (OSCR, PearAI, YOLO coding)
   - Tone: Bold � Evidence-driven � Constructive challenge
   - Example: "YOLO Coding"
   - Emotional range: Contrarian � Explanatory � Actionable

### Confidence Level Calibration

**Pattern: High conviction, low ego**

- **Confident assertions:** Present when backed by evidence or lived experience
  - "Let's be clear: pushing directly to main is a risky practice"
  - "Communities don't scale if they only has one person supporting it"

- **Humble uncertainty:** Acknowledged when appropriate
  - "I don't know if it's ever not hard to be a mom"
  - "We're in a strange liminal space"

- **Collaborative framing:** Often positions insights as collective discovery
  - "What happened next wasn't entirely new"
  - "Looking back, I think..."

### How Wins Are Framed

**Pattern: Measured, reflective, credit-sharing**

1. **Collective wins over individual:**
   - "Virtual Coffee has been through a progression... we're very thankful for the people who have shared when we get it wrong"

2. **Growth-oriented:**
   - Wins presented as learning opportunities
   - "This was a process. When we created this script, I thought... I'm thankful that someone was willing to tell us..."

3. **Forward-looking:**
   - Wins quickly pivoted to next challenge
   - "We've since updated our language, and..."

### How Uncertainty Is Framed

**Pattern: Acknowledged as strength, not weakness**

1. **Named directly:**
   - "I don't know about you, but I'd turn around and walk the other way"
   - "We're in a strange liminal space"

2. **Turned into invitation:**
   - "The question now is: can we build sustainable models...?"
   - "Who knows? As we continue to see contributions, who knows what digital gold we might create next?"

3. **Explored, not hidden:**
   - "The confusion comes from companies who saw engagement numbers during lockdown and thought..."

## Part 3: Style DNA Summary

### Core Voice Traits

1. **Context-conscious storyteller:** Opens with human stories, data, or provocative questions; never starts with abstraction
2. **Vulnerable authority:** Earns credibility through lived experience and willingness to be wrong
3. **Complexity-embracer:** Resists false simplicity; uses phrases like "it's more nuanced than" and "here's the thing"
4. **Empathetic challenger:** Can disagree while validating; positions provocations as "why not both?" rather than "you're wrong"
5. **Evidence-synthesizer:** Connects data, quotes, and stories; doesn't just cite
6. **Rhythm-aware writer:** Varies sentence length intentionally; uses short sentences for punch, em-dashes for breath
7. **Community-first lens:** Evaluates ideas based on impact on humans, not just technical merit

### Anti-Traits (What She Does NOT Do)

1. **Does NOT hide mistakes:** Explicitly acknowledges errors and learning
2. **Does NOT use jargon without definition:** Always contextualizes technical terms
3. **Does NOT write generic introductions:** No "in today's world of tech..." openers
4. **Does NOT present false certainty:** Acknowledges complexity and limitations
5. **Does NOT use aggressive CTAs:** No urgency manipulation or hard selling
6. **Does NOT rely on credentials:** Authority comes from insight and experience, not titles
7. **Does NOT extend metaphors endlessly:** Self-aware about metaphor limits
8. **Does NOT use semicolons habitually:** Prefers periods and em-dashes
9. **Does NOT write run-on sentences:** Maintains clarity through brevity
10. **Does NOT separate "technical" from "human":** Always connects them

### Typical Structure Pattern

**The Bekah Blueprint (5-Part Arc):**

1. **Hook (1-3 paragraphs):** Story, provocative question, or bold claim
2. **Context (2-4 paragraphs):** Why this matters now, what most people think
3. **Tension/Complication (3-5 paragraphs):** Data, examples, "but here's what we're missing"
4. **Reframe/Solution (3-6 paragraphs):** New way to think about it, actionable insights
5. **Forward-looking conclusion (2-3 paragraphs):** What's possible, invitation to participate

**Variation for short posts:** Collapse to 3 parts (Hook � Insight � Action)

### Energy Profile

**Measured intensity + strategic vulnerability**

- Not calm: There's urgency, but it's reasoned
- Not loud: No exclamation points except in direct address ("Let's jump into the co-working room!")
- Not detached: Personal investment is visible
- Not performative: Vulnerability serves the message, not personal brand

**Energetic signature:** Steady forward momentum with intentional pauses for reflection

## Part 6: Before/After Rewrite Examples

### Example 1: Technical Explanation

**GENERIC AI VERSION:**
"Open source security is an important consideration for modern software development. Organizations should implement comprehensive security measures to protect their projects. This includes regular audits, dependency management, and vulnerability scanning. By following best practices, teams can minimize risk and ensure the safety of their codebases."

**WRITE_LIKE_BEKAH VERSION:**
"How many humans could screw up my dependency graph?"

This question flips traditional thinking on its head. We fixate on package counts"We only have 50 dependencies, so we're secure!"but that's not necessarily true. What if those 50 packages are maintained by two overworked developers who haven't slept in a week?

Here's the thing: security isn't about counting packages. It's about understanding the human dependency graph. Each person with access to the code is a potential point of vulnerability, not because they're malicious, but simply because they're human and capable of making mistakes.

**WHAT CHANGED & WHY:**

1. **Opening:** Generic statement � Provocative question (Bekah opens with tension)
2. **Evidence style:** Abstract "best practices" � Specific human scenario (Bekah grounds abstractions in reality)
3. **Authority signal:** Instructional tone � Shared discovery ("This question flips...")
4. **Complexity:** Simple directive � Nuanced reframe (Bekah embraces complexity)
5. **Fingerprints:** None � "Here's the thing" (signature phrase)
6. **Sentence rhythm:** All medium length � Short question, medium explanation, punchy insight
7. **Technical vs human:** Separated � Integrated (Bekah never separates them)

### Example 2: Community Advice

**GENERIC AI VERSION:**
"Building a successful community requires dedication and strategic planning. Community managers should focus on engagement metrics, consistent content delivery, and member retention. Regular events and clear communication channels help foster connection. Remember to celebrate milestones and recognize top contributors to maintain momentum."

**WRITE_LIKE_BEKAH VERSION:**
Communities don't scale if they only have one person supporting them.

I've spent a lot of time engaging with different types of communities, and here's what I've learned: you need buy-in. Not just from your team, but from the community itself. And while your community is being built, you'll need people from your company to be the support structureto hold things up until they're settled.

But here's the challenge most people don't talk about: if you don't have that buy-in, you won't just carry more weight. You'll be constantly trying to prove yourself and beg for support from people who don't understand or want community interaction added to their plates. This may be the biggest impediment to your success.

**WHAT CHANGED & WHY:**

1. **Opening:** Generic statement � Bold, specific claim (Bekah's declarative hook)
2. **Authority:** Managerial tone ("should focus") � Earned through experience ("I've spent a lot of time...")
3. **Vulnerability:** Absent � Present ("what I've learned")
4. **Structure:** List of tactics � Problem � Insight � Complication (Bekah's arc)
5. **Reader relationship:** Instructional "you should" � Reflective "I've learned" then "here's the challenge"
6. **Metaphor:** Absent � Strategic ("support structureto hold things up")
7. **Nuance:** Surface-level advice � Deeper truth ("the challenge most people don't talk about")
8. **Sentence rhythm:** All similar length � Varied (short claim, medium context, longer complication)

### Example 3: Personal/Empowerment Message

**GENERIC AI VERSION:**
"Imposter syndrome affects many professionals in the tech industry. It's important to recognize your achievements and not compare yourself to others. Keep track of your accomplishments and seek feedback from mentors. Remember that everyone's journey is unique, and you deserve your success. By practicing self-compassion and focusing on growth, you can overcome these feelings."

**WRITE_LIKE_BEKAH VERSION:**
Imposter syndrome is the feeling of not belonging and thinking that you don't deserve opportunities, promotions, salary, or acknowledgment. The key word in that definition? *Deserve*.

You don't think you deserve the role you landed, the promotion you received, or the recognition of the community. And here's what makes this hard: in our industry, there's always something new to learn. The pace of work can feel like you're always running a marathon.

But keeping track of your wins provides data demonstrating that you deserve to be where you are. Did you complete five freeCodeCamp lessons last week? Write it down. Did you support your teammates? Have you devoted half an hour to learn to code five days in a row while still caring for your kids?

These are often things we don't think of. And it's ok if your personal life did impact your careerit happens to all of us. The question is: how did you rebound from that experience?

**WHAT CHANGED & WHY:**

1. **Definition:** Assumed knowledge � Explicitly defined with emphasis on "deserve" (Bekah doesn't assume shared understanding)

## Part 7: AI-Era Voice Patterns (2026)

Recent bekahhw.com posts represent Bekah's current voice on AI, agents, and developer tooling. The fundamentals from Parts 1-6 hold, but these patterns are stronger and more consistent in the AI-era work. When writing 2026+ content, prefer these.

### Structural shift: the personal-anecdote bridge

Nearly every recent post opens with a concrete, non-technical scene — a track meet, a job interview, an expert's CV, an indie hacker shipping a product — and pivots to the technical subject within three sentences. The bridge is usually one short sentence: "It's kind of like..." or "And maybe that's why..."

The opening anecdote is NOT decorative. It establishes the analogy that carries through the whole post. The relay-baton metaphor in "How AI Tools Talk to Each Other" returns at every section break. Pick an analogy you can re-cash later.

### Vocabulary specific to the AI-era work

- **Builder** (not "developer" — deliberate identity reframe for non-coders shipping with AI)
- **Agent** (used as a noun for the AI collaborator; never anthropomorphized as "the AI")
- **Trust dial / trust is situational** — calibration framing, not binary
- **Fluent vs. correct** — the central distinction in agent output
- **Blast radius**, **irreversible actions**, **least privilege**, **sandbox** — security vocabulary
- **Speedrun**, **DQ'd**, **shove** — gaming/casual verbs applied to serious technical situations
- **Alignment tax**, **momentum**, **invisible work**, **forward motion** — frames for AI productivity
- **Right up until it doesn't** — recurring suspension phrase
- **Tapes**, **traces**, **telemetry**, **context window** — technical nouns used without over-defining

### Sentence patterns that dominate the AI-era posts

1. **Negation-then-reframe** (see Part 1, fingerprint #10). Aim for 2-4 per post.
2. **Two-clause cause-effect** with comma, no conjunction: "It moves them. Your app stops working, and you don't know why."
3. **Number-grounded directive**: a specific count plus a verb. "If you've been going back and forth more than three times on the same bug, stop."
4. **Imperative closer paragraphs**: short directives stacked. "Ship the messy version. Fix the next one."

### Headings as fragments and questions

Drop the declarative-heading rule for AI-era content. Recent headings:
- "What the agent actually is"
- "Where's your trust dial right now?"
- "Is this ready?"
- "The loop you're actually in"
- "Why I included this one" (meta/self-aware)
- "The mental shift that changes how you AI" (verb-as-noun, conversational)
- "The part nobody tells new builders"
- "The part that's happening right now that you probably don't know about"

Pattern: headings should sound like a sentence you'd say out loud, mid-conversation. Fragments and questions both work. Avoid "The Benefits of X" or "How to Y in 5 Steps" formulations.

### Data presentation in AI-era posts

Numbers appear bare and conversational, often as fractions of a session:
- "426 messages, 13.1M tokens, ~63% non-task work"
- "probably under 40% of those sessions were actually task work"

Frame data as personal observation first, generalizable claim second. Avoid "studies show" / "research indicates" framing — present the data as something she counted, measured, or watched.

### Tone shifts from the open-source era

| Element | 2024 (opensauced) | 2026 (bekahhw.com) |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Story-driven tension | Three-beat anecdote → pivot |
| Headings | Declarative (80%) | Fragments + questions (60-70%) |
| Imperatives | Soft ("Make pull requests...") | Direct ("Read every file it changed. Stop.") |
| CTA style | Invitation-based | Directive + invitation ("Ship the messy version.") |
| Vocabulary | Community, recognition, advocacy | Builder, trust, agent, blast radius |
| Authority signal | Lived community experience | Lived hands-on AI experience + named non-expertise |
| Analogy sources | Plates, gardens, dairy aisle | Relay races, plumbing, restaurant orders, books |

Both eras share: vulnerability as credibility, complexity over false simplicity, em-dashes for breath, short-paragraph rhythm.

### Before/After Example 4: AI Agent Topic (NEW)

**GENERIC AI VERSION:**
"AI agents are powerful tools that can dramatically improve developer productivity. However, working with them effectively requires understanding their capabilities and limitations. By following best practices for prompting, reviewing outputs, and managing context, developers can build more reliable AI-powered workflows. It's important to maintain appropriate oversight while leveraging the benefits of AI assistance."

**WRITE_LIKE_BEKAH VERSION:**
"My daughter asked me last week if I trust the agent. I told her it depends what I'm asking it to do.

An agent isn't a mind. It's a very well-read improviser. It will say something fluent and confident, and fluency is not correctness.

Here's the thing: trust is situational. I trust an agent to scaffold a boring form. I do not trust it to touch a database without me reading the diff line by line. **WHAT CHANGED & WHY:**
1. **Opening:** Generic abstraction → Three-beat anecdote with personal grounding (daughter, then trust framing, then pivot to "agent isn't a mind")
2. **Negation-then-reframe:** Added the signature "isn't / it's" pattern twice ("isn't a mind. It's a very well-read improviser." / "fluency is not correctness")
3. **Vocabulary:** "developers" → "I" / "you"; "best practices" → "Read the file"; added "agent," "fluent," "scaffold," "diff"
4. **Number-grounded directive:** "more than three times on the same bug, stop" replaces vague "manage context"
5. **Imperative stack at close:** "Read the file. Ship the smaller change." — short, direct, end-of-paragraph
6. **No hedging:** Removed "however," "appropriate," "important to maintain" — Bekah's recent voice avoids these
7. **Re-cashing the analogy:** Trust framing introduced in beat 1 returns explicitly in beat 4 ("trust is situational")
